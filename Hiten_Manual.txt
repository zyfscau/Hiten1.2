Hiten User Manual
Hiten (high-throughput transcript end mapping system) is an organellar RNA end mapping workflow built upon the MeCi algorithm. By aligning long non-coding RNA sequencing (lncRNA-seq) data generated from in vitro circularized RNAs to both the organellar reference genomes and intron-spliced transcript sequences, Hiten identifies reads containing circle junction. Following low-confidence junction read filtering, coordinate conversion, RNA end clustering and annotation, Hiten generates a comprehensive dataset that includes high-confidence organellar RNA molecules, mapped 5′ and 3′ termini, and detailed poly(A) information.

What can Hiten do? 
(1)	Detect high-confidence organellar circle junction reads from the lncRNA-seq data of the in vitro circularized plant RNAs; 
(2)	Identify and characterize organellar poly(A) tails, including their sequences, length distributions, and nucleotide composition.
(3)	Output organellar RNA molecules and annotated 5′ and 3′ end.

Overview of the Hiten Pipeline
1. Identification of organellar circle junction reads
1.1 Perform quality control and adapter removal using cutadapt to obtain PE clean reads;
1.2 Merge the PE clean reads using FLASH, generating merged reads and unmerged reads (unmerged R1, unmerged R2);
1.3 Generate reverse-complement sequences of unmerged R1 reads using seqkit;
1.4 Combine the merged and unmerged read files using cat;
1.5 Perform a second round of quality filtering using cutadapt to remove low-quality or gaps introduced during PE-read merging;
1.6 Identify circle-junction reads using MeCi by aligning reads to both the organellar reference genomes and intron-spliced sequences.
2. Generation of high-confidence junction reads and poly(A) information
2.1 Filtering of junction reads with multiple alignments
Filtering criteria applied to both reference-genome and intron-spliced alignments:
(1)	Junctions mapping to ≥3 genomic locations are discarded;
(2)	Junctions mapping to exactly two locations are retained only if their 5′ and 3′ ends, strand orientation, and numbers of non-encoded nucleotides are identical.
2.2	Poly(A) filtering
(1)	Junction reads with 1~3 non-encoded nucleotides at the junction are fully retained.
(2)	Junction reads with 4~100 non-encoded nucleotides are retained only if ≥70% of the residues are adenosines.
2.3 Extraction of poly(A) information
After multiple alignment and poly(A) filtering, poly(A) sequences, lengths, and nucleotide compositions are extracted.
2.4 Integration of biological replicates / treatments
Results from individual datasets are combined using VLOOKUP to integrate junction-read information across replicates or treatments.
2.5 Occurrence based filtering
Only junction reads that identified at least twice across replicates or treatments are retained to generate the final set of high-confidence junction reads.
3. Coordinate conversion
3.1 Converting intron-spliced read coordinates
Using the correspondence between intron-spliced sequences and the reference genome, junction positions are converted to reference-genome coordinates.
3.2 Identification of full-length (FL) transcripts
Junction reads corresponding to FL transcripts are identified and incorporated into the reference-genome alignment results to obtain a complete set of high-confidence junction reads.
3.3 Adjusting for circular organellar genomes
Because organellar genomes are circular, reads spanning the genome-junction require coordinate correction. Using high-confidence junction read positions and non-encoded nucleotides, Hiten performs a final round of coordinate adjustment.
4. RNA-end inference, clustering, and annotation
4.1 Deriving organellar RNA molecules
Using junction read information, Hiten identifies the RNA molecule’s 5′ and 3′ ends, strand orientation, and non-encoded nucleotide additions.
4.2 Termini clustering
RNA end positions are clustered to generate final sets of organellar 5′ and 3′ termini.
4.3 Annotation of RNA ends
Using reference-genome annotations, termini are assigned to genomic features (CDS and UTR of protein-coding genes, trn genes, rrn genes, or intergenic regions).

PS:
The circRNA ID is composed of 5 parts: reference genome or chromosome, start location, end location, number of repetitive and non-encoded bases at circle junction "Z", and strand orientation.
If number Z = 0, it means the circRNA have no overlap or gap at circle junction and it has a determined location.
If number Z > 0, it means the circRNA has repetitive bases at circle junction, and the circularization site is uncertain.
   For example, MT|128095|127882|1|-, the circRNA is one of the following two locations:
   128094..127882 (5'..3' end), and 128095..127883
If number Z < 0, it means there are non-encoded bases at circle site, and the position of the circRNA is unambiguous.
   For example, MT|111285|111589|-3|+, 
   the 5' and 3' ends of this circRNA are located at 111285 and 111589, respectively, and there are 3 non-encoded bases between start and end positions of the circularization site.
